Rule Name Sub-Type How It Works Origin / Source
Active Recall Retrieval Practice Close your notes and force yourself to recall information — flashcards, practice questions, or blank-page recall. Far more effective than re-reading. Cognitive Psychology Research
Cornell Note Method Note-Taking System Divide page into 3 zones: narrow left column for keywords/questions, wide right for notes, bottom strip for summary. Review by covering notes and using cues to recall. Walter Pauk, Cornell University
Interleaving Mixed Practice Alternate between different subjects or topics in one session instead of blocking one subject for hours. Feels harder but builds deeper, more flexible understanding. Robert Bjork — Cognitive Science
Mind Mapping Visual Organisation Start with a central concept and branch out with related ideas, subtopics, and connections. Engages both visual and logical thinking for complex topics. Tony Buzan
The SQ3R Method Reading Comprehension Survey → Question → Read → Recite → Review. A structured approach to reading textbooks that turns passive reading into active learning. Francis P. Robinson
Blurting Method Brain Dump Recall Read a topic, close the book, and write down everything you remember on a blank page. Then check what you missed and repeat. Rapidly exposes gaps. Popular Exam Technique
Elaborative Interrogation Deep Understanding Ask 'Why is this true?' and 'How does this connect to what I already know?' for every new fact. Forces deeper processing beyond surface memorisation. Educational Psychology Research
Chunking Information Grouping Break large amounts of information into smaller, meaningful groups or patterns. Makes complex material easier to hold in working memory and recall later. George Miller — Cognitive Psychology
